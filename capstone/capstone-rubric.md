# Capstone Grading Rubric
**Microeconomic Theory for Experimentalists & Applied Microeconomists**

The capstone is 25–30% of the course grade (see syllabus). Within the
capstone: milestones 1–3 complete/incomplete (10%), final paper (60%),
simulation pilot + audit battery (15%), presentation (15%).

## Final paper (100 points)

| Pts | Component | Full credit looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | Question & motivation | A question two course frameworks disagree about; stakes stated for a named audience (policy, firm, literature) |
| 15 | Theory & hypotheses | Competing models generate *observably different* predictions; the design's treatments map one-to-one onto the disagreements |
| 25 | Experimental design | Subjects, treatments, and identification coherent; elicitation with an explicit incentive-compatibility argument; interference/leakage addressed where relevant; simulation-based power on human-literature effect sizes; ethics as design substance (equipoise, control-group provision, consent), not boilerplate |
| 15 | Simulation pilot | Scaffold correctly adapted; conventions intact (temperature reported, parse failures logged, prompts archived, DRY_RUN functional); preregistration filed BEFORE runs; results presented against the preregistered hypotheses |
| 15 | Comparison & divergence diagnosis | Silicon vs. human evidence compared on levels, direction, heterogeneity; every divergence placed in the taxonomy with evidence; the pre-registered decision rule executed and its outcome stated |
| 10 | The honest methods paragraph | States can-list uses performed, cannot-list uses refused, cites the stability report, sources all effect-size/power assumptions to human literature. Claiming a cannot-list use costs double its value |
| 10 | Craft | Writing a committee could read; figures that carry their weight; complete appendices (battery report, prompts, code, transcripts) |

**Standing deductions:** effect-size forecasting from silicon results
(−10); "the simulation validates the design" or equivalent (−5);
missing audit battery (−15, and the pilot component caps at half);
undisclosed AI assistance (referred to the academic-integrity policy,
not a point deduction).

## Simulation pilot + audit battery (as a separate component, 100 points)

| Pts | Component |
|---|---|
| 25 | Preregistration: complete (hypotheses, prompts, models, N, parsers, analysis, decision rule) and filed before any run |
| 25 | Execution: runs match the preregistration; deviations disclosed and justified |
| 25 | Audit battery: ≥2 models × ≥3 verified content-preserving paraphrases × ≥2 temperatures; report read correctly against stated stability thresholds |
| 25 | Claims discipline: the claims ladder filled honestly; surviving claims match the evidence (over-claiming costs double) |

## Presentation (100 points)

| Pts | Component |
|---|---|
| 25 | The arc: question → design → what the pilot changed → the refined proposal (a listener can reconstruct all four) |
| 25 | Design fluency: answers about identification, IC, power, and ethics without retreating to slides |
| 30 | The steel-man exchange: restates the objection at full strength, concedes what is right, defends only what survives — with a concrete battery result |
| 20 | Craft: timing (12 min), legible slides, figures over text |

## What distinguishes an A capstone
Not a positive result — there are no results yet; that is the point.
An A capstone has: a question worth the committee's hour; treatments
that make theories disagree in data; an incentive-compatibility
argument a skeptic would accept; a pilot that *changed something
specific* about the design (wording, an arm added or dropped, a
measurement sharpened) with the change traced to evidence; and an
honest paragraph that would survive this course's own blind review.
